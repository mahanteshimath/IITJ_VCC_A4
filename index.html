Lab 1

Processor Privilege Rings and Control

Move actions through the platform stack and compare containers, full virtualization, and hardware-assisted virtualization.

What this lab teaches

  • Why ordinary apps stay in Ring 3 and need system calls for protected work.
  • Why drivers and kernel-level code get direct control, not just access.
  • How hypervisors gain extra power and how VT-x or AMD-V introduces a root-mode interception path.
  • Why deleting a file removes metadata first and physical overwrite later.

Mental model

Treat the machine like a building with layers of keys. Apps can request a room service. Drivers and kernels can open locked utility closets. Hypervisors can stand above guest operating systems and intercept critical actions before they touch hardware.

Lab 2

Distributed Management Architecture

Toggle coordination styles, trigger network changes, and watch how updates propagate through leaders, trackers, and clients.

What this lab teaches

  • Client-server is simple to coordinate, but leaders and primaries become failure pressure points.
  • Peer-to-peer systems still need central discovery or bootstrap helpers in practice.
  • Push delivery only works after a client first registers itself through an earlier pull-like contact.
  • Logical clocks preserve event order even when physical clocks disagree.

Mental model

Picture a restaurant chain. A leader kitchen coordinates recipes and stock, clients place orders, replicas cover failures, and even the "decentralized" version still relies on a shared member list to find peers.

    Lab 3

    CAP Theorem and Consistency Models

    Split the network, choose CP or AP behavior, and compare freshness guarantees across common consistency models.

    What this lab teaches

    • Partition tolerance is not optional in cloud systems, because real networks eventually fail.
    • CP systems preserve a single truth by rejecting some requests during a split.
    • AP systems stay responsive during a split but may return stale or diverged data until reconciliation.
    • Strong, eventual, causal, monotonic, and read-your-writes guarantees serve different product needs.

    Mental model

    A partition is like cutting the hallway between offices. Teams on both sides can keep working, but now you have to decide whether they pause for agreement or continue with possibly conflicting versions.
